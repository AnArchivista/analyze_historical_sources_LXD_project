note: 

The collection is catalogued as "Federal Writers' Project Narratives of Formerly Enslaved Peoples Collection (GV017)".  

It is important to note that the term "formerly enslaved" is a contemporary term used to counter the dehumanization of the label "slave" relating to chattel slavery. 

Chattel differed from other forms of slavery or indentured servitude in that the enslaved person and all subsequent descendants were socially marked as property of the enslavers, for all generations to come.  

However, when conducting historical research it is often necessary to encounter and use search terms that are offensive or dehumanizing.  These FWP interviews are typically located by using search terms that were originally assigned to the material, i.e. "ex-slave narratives".

Explicit information

Interviewer Carrie Campbell's...

Source Analysis and Interpretation



Section 7

It is often necessary to conduct research for addtional information to better inform an analysis of a historical source.

This is also necessary to move from historical source analysis to historical source interpretation.  

Analysis focuses more on explicit information, while interpretation typically requires using implicit information to form an interpretation.  All iterpretations are influenced by a person's background, knowledge, experiences, etc. and can differ from person to person.  It is imperative to point to fact-based source analyses to lend credibility to a given interpretation.  


Search results for 221 Court Street Clarksdale, Mississippi:

Items D1 & D2



Analysis based on

Explicit information:

  • 221 Court Street where Carrie Cambpell recieved mail from the WPA in the 1930's, has a house that was built in 1910.

Analysis based in implicit information:

  • 221 Court Street, was either Carrie Campbell's permanent residence, or she was a house guest or in the very least she was allowed to recieve mail there. 


Consider the example interpretation again:

"It is possible that Calline Brown considered Carrie Campbell to be part of an affluent 'middle' or 'upper' economic class, because Calline Brown (a Black woman) in 1937, felt comfortable using the term 'poor White trash' when being interviewed by Carrie Campbell (a White woman)."

Do these sources corroborate or contradict the example interpretation?

  • These sources do not provide conclusive information to corroborate or contradict the example interpretation.


Historical research requires asking questions.  Even questions that are unable to be difinitively answered can offer insight.

  • What was Calline Brown's social and economic status?
  • What was Carrie Campbell's social and economic status?
  • Would a difference in socio-economic status between the interviewer and interviewee effect the interview?
  • Was Carrie Campbell a house guest or permanent resident at 221 Court Street?
  • Did Carrie Campbell have afluent social and economic status prior to the Great Depression?  
    • If so, did the Great Depression change that?  
      • If so, how so?  If not, why not?  
  • Did Carrie Campbell demostrate need of WPA employment services?
